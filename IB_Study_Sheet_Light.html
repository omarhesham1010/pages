Lectures 1 & 2

Fundamentals of International Business

IB vs Domestic
IWE
MDC

What is International Business?

  • International Business (IB): activities carried across national borders.
  • Foreign Business: operations of a company outside its home market.
  • Multi‑Domestic Company (MDC): has affiliates per country, each crafting its own strategy for local differences.

International Work Environment (IWE)

  • People from different nationalities work together; global branches/clients.
  • Relies on global standards, virtual collaboration, cross‑border operations.
  • Builds intercultural communication & adaptability.

Exam Nuggets

IB vs Domestic Business
Domestic faces mainly home forces; IB faces domestic, foreign, and international forces together.
Why managers need IB literacy?
  • Firms increasingly engaged in IB; CEOs expect global perspective.
  • Important for senior and entry roles; critical as activity expands.
Forces

Environment & Forces

Uncontrollable (External) Forces

  • Competitive, Distributive, Economic, Socioeconomic, Financial
  • Legal, Physical, Political, Sociocultural, Labor, Technological

Controllable (Internal) Forces

  • Strategy, structure, systems, resources that management can adjust.

Environments

  • Domestic (home‑country external forces)
  • Foreign (host‑country external forces)
  • International (interaction among sets of forces)

Motives to Enter Foreign Markets

  • Grow sales & profits; faster‑growing markets; lower costs.
  • Secure supply/technology; diversify geographically.
  • Protect/defend markets; follow customers; attack competitors at home.
Trends

Globalization

Definition & Drivers

  • Integration of goods, tech, info, labor, capital.
  • Drivers: Political, Technological, Market, Cost, Competitive.

Support vs Concerns

  • Pros Growth, efficiency, jobs, tech transfer, lower prices, innovation.
  • Concerns Inequality, labor & environment issues, cultural loss, dependency.

Facets

  • Markets merge; production sourced globally.
  • Impacts jobs/income, policy, sovereignty, the poor.
Globalization of Markets vs Production
  • Markets: converging tastes → one big marketplace (e.g., Coke, PlayStation).
  • Production: source where cost/quality of factors best (e.g., Boeing, Vizio).
Institutions

International Institutions

Why Institutions Matter

  • Enforce rules; reduce barriers; support fair competition.
  • Set technical/safety standards; prevent trade chaos.

Types

  • Regional (EU), Nongovernmental (ICC), Intergovernmental (WTO, IMF, World Bank, UN).

WTO

  • Founded 1995; 160+ members; manages global trade rules.
  • Functions: reduce barriers, dispute resolution, negotiations, monitoring.

UN Family

  • IMF: currency stability, balance‑of‑payments support, exchange rate oversight.
  • World Bank: development & poverty reduction, infrastructure, capacity building.
  • UN: peace, standards, development agendas.
Critiques & Cases
  • Bias toward wealthy; transparency issues (Seattle 1999); slow reform (IMF voting).
  • Example: WTO Banana dispute; Zambia outcomes of structural reforms.
Lecture 6

Political & Legal Forces

Government & Competition

  • Nationalization: state takes control (revenue, ideology, jobs, post‑event).
  • Privatization: shift to private sector to boost efficiency.
  • Unfair Competition: false ads, trademark abuse, trade‑secret theft, dumping, predatory pricing.

Government Protection

  • Economic (tariffs, subsidies), Consumer, Environmental, Labor, National Security.

Country Risk Assessment

  • Political, Economic, Legal/Regulatory, FX risk, Transfer/Convertibility.

Legal Forces

  • Contracts, tax, labor, trade limits, environment, IP, consumer protection.
  • Dispute resolution: courts vs arbitration (ICC) – faster, neutral, enforceable.

IP & Compliance

  • IP types: patents, copyrights, trademarks, trade secrets.
  • Anti‑corruption: FCPA (US), UK Bribery Act; heavy penalties.
  • Tax: transfer pricing scrutiny; double‑tax treaties.

Dumping – Types

  • Persistent, Predatory, Sporadic, Social/Environmental, Reverse, Financial, Tax.
Sociocultural

Sociocultural Forces

What is Culture?

  • Values + Norms shared by a group → “design for living”.
  • Affects all functions: marketing, HR, operations, controls, leadership.

Determinants & Workplace

  • Social class, religion/ethics, language (spoken & unspoken), education.
  • Hofstede: power distance, individualism, uncertainty avoidance, masculinity.

Implications

  • Cross‑cultural literacy; culture ↔ competitive advantage; ethical decisions.
Examples – Hofstede
  • High power distance: Egypt; Low: Sweden.
  • Individualism: USA; Collectivism: China.
  • High UA: Greece; Low UA: Singapore.
  • Masculinity: Japan; Femininity: Netherlands.
